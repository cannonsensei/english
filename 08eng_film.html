The Silent Era — c. 1890s to 1920s

Cinema was born in the 1890s. On 28 December 1895, Auguste and Louis Lumière held the first public film screening at the Grand Café in Paris, projecting short documentary films of everyday life — workers leaving a factory, a train arriving at a station. Within months, filmmakers across the world were experimenting with the new medium, drawn by its capacity to capture and replay movement.

Early cinema evolved rapidly from simple recordings of movement into complex narrative art. Georges Méliès pioneered special effects and fantasy storytelling; D.W. Griffith developed the grammar of film editing, including the close-up and cross-cutting; and Sergei Eisenstein theorised montage as a political and emotional tool. By the 1920s, filmmakers had established many of the visual conventions still in use today — though all without synchronised sound, which would not arrive until The Jazz Singer in 1927.

Key Films

Key Figures

1902

A Trip to the Moon

Directed by Georges Méliès. A 14-minute fantasy film using hand-painted frames, stop-tricks, and theatrical staging. Widely considered the first science fiction film.

1861–1938

Georges Méliès

French filmmaker and stage magician who discovered the stop-trick accidentally and went on to make over 500 films. His purpose-built studio at Montreuil was among the first in the world.

1920

The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari

Directed by Robert Wiene. A German Expressionist masterpiece whose distorted, angular painted sets expressed psychological horror visually. Enormously influential on horror and film noir.

1889–1977

Charlie Chaplin

British-born actor, director, and composer who became one of cinema's first global stars through his "Tramp" character. Co-founded United Artists in 1919 to protect creative independence.

1925

The Gold Rush

Directed by and starring Charlie Chaplin. The Tramp seeks fortune in the Klondike. Contains iconic sequences including the eating of a boot and the Oceana Roll dance performed with bread rolls.

1862–1954 / 1864–1948

The Lumière Brothers

Auguste and Louis Lumière invented the Cinématographe — a combined camera, printer, and projector. Their 1895 Paris screening is widely regarded as the birth of cinema as a public art form.

The Hollywood Golden Age — c. 1930s to 1960s

By the 1930s, Hollywood had developed the studio system — a model in which major studios including MGM, Warner Bros., Paramount, RKO, and 20th Century Fox controlled production, distribution, and exhibition. Studios signed actors, directors, and writers to long-term contracts, creating an efficient and prolific factory for popular entertainment. At its peak, Hollywood was producing over 500 films per year.

The Golden Age produced many of cinema's most enduring works. The arrival of synchronised sound in the late 1920s had transformed the industry, and by the late 1930s Technicolor was enabling lavish productions such as Gone with the Wind and The Wizard of Oz (both 1939). Established genres — the western, the musical, the screwball comedy, and film noir — defined the era. Content was governed by the Production Code (the Hays Code), which enforced strict moral guidelines on subject matter from 1934 until its collapse in the late 1960s.

Key Films

Key Figures

1941

Citizen Kane

Directed by Orson Welles (aged 25 during production). Its innovative use of deep focus photography, non-linear narrative, and unconventional low-angle shots redefined cinematic technique. Consistently ranked among the greatest films ever made.

1915–1985

Orson Welles

Actor, director, and producer who made Citizen Kane at 25. His career was marked by battles with studios over creative control, but his influence on visual storytelling — particularly his use of deep focus and long takes — is immeasurable.

1942

Casablanca

Directed by Michael Curtiz. Set during World War II, the film won three Academy Awards including Best Picture. Celebrated for its screenplay, performances by Humphrey Bogart and Ingrid Bergman, and its atmospheric cinematography.

1899–1980

Alfred Hitchcock

British-born director known as "the Master of Suspense." Made 53 feature films including Vertigo (1958), Psycho (1960), and North by Northwest (1959). Pioneered the use of the subjective camera and psychological tension.

1954

Rear Window

Directed by Alfred Hitchcock. A photographer confined to a wheelchair believes he has witnessed a murder through his apartment window. A masterwork of suspense and a meditation on the voyeuristic nature of cinema itself.

1906–2002

Billy Wilder

Austrian-born director and screenwriter. His films — including Double Indemnity (1944), Sunset Boulevard (1950), and Some Like It Hot (1959) — are celebrated for their sharp dialogue, dark wit, and moral complexity.

New Wave & International Cinema — c. 1950s to 1970s

The New Wave refers to a series of film movements in the late 1950s and 1960s that rejected classical Hollywood conventions in favour of location shooting, improvisation, handheld cameras, and personal filmmaking. The French Nouvelle Vague was led by critics-turned-directors — Jean-Luc Godard, François Truffaut, Claude Chabrol, Éric Rohmer — who had written for the journal Cahiers du Cinéma. They championed the auteur theory: the idea that the director is the primary author of a film, stamping it with a personal vision as a writer stamps a novel.

Beyond France, this era saw the global rise of important national cinemas. Japanese cinema gave the world Akira Kurosawa and Yasujirō Ozu; Italian cinema produced Federico Fellini and Michelangelo Antonioni; Swedish cinema produced Ingmar Bergman. Meanwhile in America, "New Hollywood" directors including Francis Ford Coppola, Martin Scorsese, and Robert Altman brought European art cinema sensibilities into mainstream studio filmmaking, producing some of the most complex and challenging American films ever made.

Key Films

Key Figures

1960

Breathless (À bout de souffle)

Jean-Luc Godard's debut feature. Shot on location in Paris with a handheld camera, it deliberately used jump cuts to break Hollywood's smooth continuity editing. A foundational text of the Nouvelle Vague.

1930–2022

Jean-Luc Godard

Swiss-French filmmaker and one of the most influential directors of the 20th century. His radical approach to editing, narrative structure, and cinema's relationship with politics transformed filmmaking worldwide.

1963

8½

Federico Fellini's self-reflexive masterpiece about a film director in creative crisis. It blends memory, fantasy, and reality in a way that was entirely new, and has influenced filmmakers from Woody Allen to Paolo Sorrentino.

1920–1993

Federico Fellini

Italian director whose films are characterised by dreamlike imagery, autobiographical content, and a carnivalesque visual style. Won four Academy Awards for Best Foreign Language Film — a record at the time.

1954

Seven Samurai

Akira Kurosawa's epic about a group of samurai hired to protect a village. Its three-and-a-half-hour narrative structure and dynamic action sequences directly inspired The Magnificent Seven (1960) and countless action films since.

1910–1998

Akira Kurosawa

Japanese director who made 30 films over five decades, bridging Eastern and Western storytelling traditions. George Lucas, Francis Ford Coppola, and Steven Spielberg have all cited him as a defining influence on their work.

Modern & Contemporary Film — c. 1980s to present

The modern era of Hollywood began with the rise of the blockbuster. Jaws (1975) and Star Wars (1977) demonstrated the commercial power of high-concept films released widely and marketed aggressively — a model that reshaped the industry and continues to dominate it today. At the same time, a generation of directors trained on European and Asian art cinema — Scorsese, Coppola, De Palma — brought formal ambition to mainstream studio filmmaking, producing complex and challenging American films.

Digital technology transformed cinema from the 1990s onward. CGI redefined what was possible on screen, with Jurassic Park (1993) and The Matrix (1999) as landmarks. Independent cinema flourished: Quentin Tarantino's Pulp Fiction (1994) and the Coen Brothers' work showed that formally adventurous filmmaking could reach wide audiences. Most recently, the global expansion of the film industry has seen South Korean, Mexican, and other international filmmakers achieve major recognition. Bong Joon-ho's Parasite (2019) became the first non-English-language film to win the Academy Award for Best Picture — a historic marker of cinema's increasingly global reach.

Key Films

Key Figures

1993

Schindler's List

Steven Spielberg's account of Oskar Schindler, a German industrialist who saved over 1,000 Jewish people during the Holocaust. Shot in black and white, it won seven Academy Awards including Best Picture and Best Director.

b. 1946

Steven Spielberg

American director, producer, and screenwriter whose films have collectively grossed more than those of any other director in history. Key works include Jaws, E.T., Raiders of the Lost Ark, Schindler's List, and Saving Private Ryan.

2000

Memento

Christopher Nolan's psychological thriller told in reverse chronological order. Its structure forces the viewer to experience the protagonist's memory loss firsthand, making form and content inseparable.

b. 1970

Christopher Nolan

British-American director known for films that challenge linear time and perception. Key works include Memento, The Dark Knight trilogy, Inception, Interstellar, Dunkirk, and Oppenheimer (2023).

2019

Parasite

Bong Joon-ho's darkly comic thriller about class inequality in contemporary South Korea. Won four Academy Awards including Best Picture — the first non-English-language film to achieve this.

b. 1969

Bong Joon-ho

South Korean director whose films blend genre conventions with sharp social commentary. His international breakthrough came with The Host (2006); Parasite (2019) brought him global recognition and four Academy Awards.

Analysis in Film: Inside Out 2

In this topic we will work through a modelled process of analysing film features to support a thesis.

Our focus film is Inside Out 2 (dir. Kelsey Mann, 2024, Pixar/Disney).

The purpose of this work is to consolidate your understanding of how analysing film features to support an interpretation looks, and the skills involved in producing cogent analysis.

02
Context
What's Inside Out 2 about?

One of the central themes of Inside Out 2 is the emotional upheaval that accompanies adolescence.

As Riley transitions from childhood to her teenage years, we witness the introduction of new emotions — Anxiety, Envy, Embarrassment, and Ennui (Boredom) — which reflect the increasingly complex emotional landscape teens must navigate.

03
Big ideas
The focus of our study: growing up
Emotional complexity
As Riley enters adolescence, her emotions become more nuanced, reflecting the increasing complexity of human feelings.
Role of anxiety
Anxiety highlights how worry and self-doubt shape our decisions, but also how they can be balanced with other emotions.
Identity formation
Inside Out 2 explores how identity evolves in adolescence, showing how emotions influence our sense of self as we grow.
★ Our focus
Emotional integration — by the film's resolution, different emotions coexist and contribute to Riley's sense-of-self.

Key terms

The process of acknowledging, understanding, and using your emotions in a balanced way. It can help you make better decisions and have better relationships.
Sense of self
Your perception of the collection of characteristics that define you — personality traits, abilities, likes and dislikes, your belief system or moral code, and the things that motivate you.

Worked example
Analysis of dialogue
The phrase "keep the best, toss the rest" is a line of dialogue spoken by Joy signifying her approach to managing Riley's memories, where she actively selects and stores the positive experiences while discarding the less favourable ones. This line of dialogue reflects her character's tendency to prioritise happiness and optimism. Yet, while Joy's insistence that positivity is the key to wellbeing positions audiences to recognise her genuine care for Riley, her dialogue also conveys a misguided approach to happiness, one that favours emotional suppression rather than integration.

Mic drop.

15
Full paragraph
Argument Paragraph 1 — complete
At the beginning of Inside Out 2, Riley's initial struggle stems from her mind's attempt to suppress unpleasant emotions like Anxiety and Embarrassment, leading to internal conflict and emotional imbalance. In an important opening scene, the character Joy introduces a machine designed to throw bad memories to the back of Riley's as a way to protect her from negative emotions. In this scene, Joy describes her approach by announcing, "keep the best, toss the rest." This line of dialogue spoken by Joy signifies her approach to managing Riley's memories, where she actively selects and stores the positive experiences while discarding the less favourable ones. In this way, the film reflects Joy's tendency to prioritise happiness and optimism over all else. Yet, while Joy's insistence that positivity is the key to wellbeing positions audiences to recognise her genuine care for Riley, her dialogue also conveys a misguided approach to happiness, one that favours emotional suppression rather than integration.
